# History of Segment Trees

## Origins in Computational Geometry (1970s)

The segment tree was invented by Jon Louis Bentley in 1977 as part of his work on computational geometry. He published the data structure in his paper "Algorithms for the fixed-radius neighbor problem." Bentley was looking for efficient ways to store and query intervals for geometric algorithms.

## Early Applications (1977-1985)

- **Line sweep algorithms**: Segment trees became the backbone of plane sweep techniques
- **Rectangle intersection**: Used for VLSI design rule checking
- **Database indexing**: Early database systems adopted interval trees and segment trees

## Variations and Extensions (1985-2000)

- **Lazy Propagation**: The concept of lazy propagation was formalized in the late 1980s, significantly expanding the utility of segment trees for range update problems
- **2D Segment Trees**: Extensions to multi-dimensional space for spatial queries
- **Dynamic Segment Trees**: Support for dynamic insertion and deletion of points
- **Persistent Segment Trees**: Allowing queries on previous versions (useful for offline algorithms)

## Modern Era (2000-Present)

- **Competitive Programming**: Segment trees became one of the most important data structures in algorithms competitions (ICPC, IOI, Codeforces)
- **Iterative Segment Trees**: Optimized array-based implementations for better performance
- **Small-to-Large Merging**: Combining segment trees for tree problems
- **ZKW Segment Trees**: Named after Zhang Ka Wai, a memory-efficient iterative implementation
