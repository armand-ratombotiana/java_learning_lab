# History of Exact Exponential Algorithms

1974: Richard Karp introduced the concept of exact exponential algorithms for NP-hard problems in his landmark paper "Reducibility among Combinatorial Problems."

1980s: The meet-in-the-middle technique was used for knapsack and subset sum problems. Horowitz and Sahni achieved O(2^{n/2}) for subset sum.

1990s: Inclusion-exclusion techniques were applied to exact exponential algorithms by Ryser (for the permanent) and Bjorklund (for Hamiltonian path).

2004: Husfeldt and colleagues discovered a O(1.618^n) algorithm for vertex cover using branching rules. This marked the beginning of modern exact exponential algorithms.

2008: Ryan Williams introduced fast subset convolution, achieving O(n^2 * 2^n) for subset convolution, which improved many DP-on-subsets algorithms.

2010: Bjorklund and colleagues published a breakthrough O(1.657^n) algorithm for the k-path problem using inclusion-exclusion and fast zeta transforms.

2012: Andreas Bjorklund, Thore Husfeldt, and Petteri Kaski developed the split-and-list method, further reducing exponential bases for several problems.

2015: Exact algorithms for SAT achieved O(1.307^n) via the PPSZ algorithm. The exponential-time hypothesis (ETH) and strong ETH provided a framework for understanding optimal exponential algorithms.

2020s: Exact exponential algorithms remain an active research area with applications in computational biology, cryptography, and operations research.
